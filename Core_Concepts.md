# Core Concepts

Understanding these concepts helps explain *why* the recommendations later in the guide matter.

---

## End-to-End Encryption (E2EE)

End-to-end encryption means that data is encrypted **on your device before it is sent**, and only decrypted by the intended recipient. The service provider itself cannot read the content.

This is important because it protects your data even if:
- The provider is hacked
- Employees access internal systems
- The provider is legally compelled to hand over stored data

Examples of E2EE include:
- Messaging apps like Signal
- Encrypted cloud storage like Proton Drive
- Email between users on the same encrypted email provider

A good rule of thumb is to prefer services where encryption happens **before data leaves your device**, rather than only on the server.

---

## Security Keys

A security key is a small physical device (USB, NFC, or Lightning) used to confirm your identity during login.

Security keys are highly resistant to phishing because they verify that you are logging into the *real* website, not a fake one.

For beginners:
- Security keys are **not mandatory**
- They are an excellent upgrade after basic protections are in place
- They are especially valuable for email accounts and password managers

---

## Virtual Private Networks (VPNs)

A VPN encrypts your internet traffic and routes it through another server before reaching the destination.

A VPN can:
- Protect your traffic on public Wi-Fi
- Hide your IP address from websites
- Reduce ISP-level tracking

A VPN does **not**:
- Make you anonymous
- Protect against malware
- Replace good passwords or encryption

VPNs are a situational tool, not a universal solution.

---

## Time-Based One-Time Passcodes (TOTP)

TOTP codes are short numeric codes (usually 6 digits) that change every 30 seconds. They are generated by an authenticator app.

These codes dramatically reduce the risk of account takeover, even if your password is stolen.

They are widely supported by major services such as email providers, social networks, and developer platforms.

---

## Two-Factor Authentication (2FA)

Two-factor authentication combines:
- Something you know (a password)
- Something you have (a phone, authenticator app, or security key)

Strength ranking from strongest to weakest:
1. Security keys
2. Authenticator apps (TOTP)
3. SMS codes (better than nothing)

Whenever possible, avoid SMS-only 2FA.
